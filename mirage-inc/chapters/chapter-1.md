# Chapter 1 — Roommates

Jake Miller stood in the doorway of dormitory 214 and took it in — the room was exactly the size of a mistake. A loft bed jutting from the left wall with two desks crammed underneath it, a bunk bed against the opposite wall, one window on the far side facing the parking lot, walls the color of institutional indifference, a ceiling light that flickered once when he hit the switch as if registering its own protest. His father had offered to rent him an apartment off campus. Jake had declined on principle, a principle that was already feeling less solid than it had three hours ago.

He dropped his duffel on the lower bunk and went to the window. The parking lot below was full of cars and parents and the specific anxious energy of people trying to look like they weren't crying. Move-in day at UC Berkeley. September in Northern California, which meant the heat was still thick and golden and the hills behind campus were the dry brown that people from other states always found surprising. Jake had grown up thirty minutes east in Walnut Creek and thought nothing of it.

His phone buzzed. His father.

*Proud of you, son. First step toward something great.*

Jake put the phone face-down on the desk and went to unpack.

He had been working on the second box — clothes, mostly, and a framed photo of himself and two friends from high school at the beach, everyone squinting into the sun — when the door opened and his first roommate arrived.

The person who came through the door was medium height, slightly built, wearing a t-shirt with a faded logo Jake didn't recognize. He was carrying a single large duffel and a backpack and a box that rattled in a way that suggested it contained something that had once been a computer and had been disassembled for transport. He looked at the room with the expression of someone running a rapid spatial calculation, set everything down with the careful economy of someone who had thought about exactly where things were going before he entered, and then looked at Jake.

"I took the lower bunk," Jake said.

"Upper bunk is fine." He put out a hand. "Mike Bondarenko."

"Jake Miller."

They shook. Mike's grip was precise rather than strong, the handshake of someone who had been taught to do it correctly rather than someone who did it constantly. He was already looking at the desk, the window, the dimensions of the shared space, cataloguing.

"CS?" Jake asked.

"EECS." Mike nodded and started unpacking.

Jake looked at his own two boxes and the duffel bag and went back to unpacking.

They worked in parallel for a while, the room gradually becoming more inhabited around them. Mike claimed the desk under the loft — the darker of the two positions but with more wall space — and organized it with the precise density of someone who had planned this before arriving. A desk setup assembled from components he had apparently packed himself, two small plants placed on the narrow windowsill with the confidence of someone who had already decided that was where they were going. He threw his bag onto the upper bunk without ceremony and went back to work. Jake's side of the lower bunk looked like a person lived there, which he supposed was the same thing achieved by different methods. The loft bed above the two remaining desks sat empty, waiting.

The third roommate arrived an hour later.

He came through the door already talking — not to anyone specifically, just talking, the way some people think out loud at full volume. He was carrying one large suitcase and a backpack and a plastic bag from a pharmacy near the airport that he set down in the middle of the floor and then gestured at broadly.

"I bought things at the airport. Very expensive, bro. Everything at airports is very expensive — why is this?" He looked at Jake and then Mike with the open expectation of someone who genuinely wanted an answer. "I'm Arthur. Arthur Mkhitaryan. I have the loft."

"Jake."

"Mike."

They shook. Then a beat of silence.

"Sorry," Jake said. "Miki... Mikitar—"

"Mkhitaryan," Arthur said.

"Mikitaryan."

"Mkhitaryan."

"That's what I said."

Arthur looked at him for a moment. "It is close enough," he said, in the tone of someone who had been having this conversation his entire life and had made peace with it.

Arthur shook both their hands with tremendous enthusiasm and then began unpacking with a speed and disorder that was the precise opposite of Mike's method — things came out of the suitcase in no particular sequence and were placed on surfaces according to some logic that was not immediately apparent. A laptop — Windows, a large gaming model with a sticker of the Armenian flag on the lid — went on the window desk first, which he had apparently assessed as his without discussion. Then clothes. Then a framed photo of what appeared to be a very large extended family. Then three phone chargers. He climbed the loft ladder twice to test it, declared it acceptable, and climbed back down.

"CS?" Jake asked.

"Yes, bro! Also you?" Arthur looked delighted.

"CS."

They both looked at Mike.

"EECS," Mike said, without looking up from his unpacking.

Arthur stared at him for a moment. "Bro." He said it with the weight of a genuine compliment. Mike's expression suggested he had received this reaction before and had decided it was acceptable. "All three of us — technical. This is good. We can help each other." He looked at Mike's precisely organized desk and then at Jake's considerably less populated one.

"Not yet," Mike said.

Arthur was from Yerevan — Armenia's capital, he explained, though he had lived his whole life near the university district there and was therefore no stranger to students. He had a scholarship from the Armenian government and a partial sponsorship from a foundation in the diaspora community, which together covered enough of Berkeley's fees to make attendance possible if not comfortable. He had never been to California before. He had very specific opinions about the airport coffee.

"How did you end up here specifically?" Jake asked. "Berkeley."

"I applied to twelve universities. Berkeley accepted me." He said it simply, the way Mike said things that were just true. "Also I have a cousin in Glendale. So California was — it made sense." He plugged in the first of his three chargers. "You are from here?"

"Walnut Creek. Thirty minutes east."

"And you?" He looked at Mike.

"Kansas City."

"Missouri." Arthur nodded as if filing this away. "I don't know Kansas City. Is it good?"

"It's where I'm from," Mike said, which was not exactly an answer but Arthur accepted it as one.

When Mike finished unpacking he said he was going to find the common room. Jake followed him. Arthur followed both of them, still talking.

The common room was down the hall — a wide space with couches, vending machines, and a kitchenette with a drip coffee machine that looked like it had been there since the building opened. Mike studied the kitchenette with the focus of someone making a permanent decision. He opened the cupboards, measured the counter space with his eyes, and after a moment said he was going to order a hot plate and a kettle.

"For tea," he added.

"Is that allowed?" Jake asked.

"I'll put it in the common room. Nobody will object."

"I also want something," Arthur said, looking at the kitchenette. "I will think about what." He opened the refrigerator and examined its contents. "Vay — this refrigerator has a lock. Who locked the refrigerator?"

Nobody knew. Arthur looked at this as an interesting problem rather than an obstacle, which Jake was starting to understand was simply how he looked at things.

"There's a coffee place on Telegraph that opens at six," Mike said. "Better than the machine."

"You looked that up before you got here," Jake said.

"I look things up before I go to them."

"I also looked things up," Arthur said. "On Shattuck Avenue there is an Armenian restaurant. Very good reviews. We should go, bro." He looked between Jake and Mike. "Friday maybe? Yes?"

Jake looked at Mike. Mike looked at the coffee machine with the expression of someone deciding something.

"Sure," Jake said. "Friday."

Arthur looked pleased in the uncomplicated way of someone for whom a solved problem stays solved. He went back to the room to finish unpacking, still talking, his voice receding down the hall.

Jake leaned against the counter and considered the situation — the plants, the already-formed plan about the kettle, the Armenian restaurant on Shattuck — and decided that this was going to be interesting in one direction or another.

---

They found their equilibrium without discussing it, the way people who share a small space eventually do.

Jake went out. Mike stayed in. Arthur did both simultaneously, somehow — he was at his desk when Jake left for parties and occasionally still there when Jake came back, but he had also been to three club meetings and introduced himself to everyone on the floor and resolved a disagreement between two sophomores in the room next door about a borrowed charger, all apparently in the same evening. He also disappeared occasionally for fifteen minutes at a time, always returned slightly more settled, and Jake eventually worked out that he walked to the street just outside the campus boundary to smoke — Berkeley being entirely smoke-free, campus and dorms both, which Arthur had apparently accepted as simply how things were here without complaint.

Jake made it to the campus rec center a few mornings a week when he felt like it, which was often enough to stay in shape and not so often that it felt like a commitment. Mike woke at seven, walked to the common room in whatever he had slept in, made the first tea of the day on the hot plate he had installed on day three without anyone objecting, and spent the first hour of every morning studying before showering. Arthur woke at eight-fifteen, located his phone, and immediately began texting someone — family, Jake assumed, given the time difference — for twenty minutes before getting up.

Jake ate whatever the dining hall was serving. Mike had a rotation of places on Telegraph Avenue he had identified in the first two weeks. Arthur ate everywhere, enthusiastically, and always had an opinion about it afterward.

On Friday nights Jake went to parties — came back smelling like cheap beer, sometimes louder than the hour warranted, always fine by morning. Mike ordered from the Thai place three blocks away and worked on whatever he was working on with the quiet contentment of someone in exactly the right place. He never drank, never seemed to want to, and never mentioned it either way. Arthur went to the Armenian restaurant on Shattuck and usually came back with food for whoever was in the room, whether they had asked for it or not — containers of things that smelled extraordinary, set on the desk without ceremony, available to anyone.

They talked in the margins, around other things, the way people who share a space naturally do. Jake learned that Mike had wanted to do EECS since he was twelve, had taken apart every appliance in the apartment growing up to understand the mechanics, had a mother who eventually started leaving things out for him to disassemble once she understood what he was doing — a woman with an engineering degree of her own who recognized the instinct even if she hadn't had much use for it lately. Mike had grown up in Kansas City, Missouri. He had flown to San Francisco for the first time in September, one checked bag, a ThinkPad he had repaired twice, and an EECS acceptance letter he had worked four years to earn. Mike learned that Jake was in CS because his father had wanted an engineer, that Jake had not disagreed strongly because he hadn't yet found something to strongly agree with instead.

"That's not really a reason to be somewhere," Mike said one evening, not looking up from his work.

"I know," Jake said.

"What do you actually want to do?"

Jake thought about it honestly. "I don't know yet."

Mike nodded as if this were a complete and reasonable answer and went back to his work. Arthur, who was at his own desk, looked up briefly and then decided this was not a conflict requiring resolution and went back to whatever he was doing.

Jake sat with the question for a while, which he had been doing more often lately.

His phone buzzed. His father again: *How are classes? Let's catch up this weekend.*

Jake replied: *Good. Busy. Maybe next weekend.*

He put the phone down. His father worked at Mirage Inc — had worked there since before Jake was born, operations manager, a solid position in the middle of the org chart. The kind of person who understood how a large company ran without being involved in why it existed. He came home at six, ate dinner, watched the game, went to bed. He talked about his job the way people talked about weather. Jake had grown up with the company's name in the background of his life without ever thinking much about what the name meant.

---

He thought about it for the first time in October, three weeks into the semester, when Mike mentioned Mirage Inc.

They were in the common room after dinner, Jake with his coffee and Mike with something that smelled like toasted rice, both of them nominally doing readings that neither was fully concentrating on. Arthur had been there earlier but had gone to resolve something — there was always something — and the room was quiet.

Mike had been quiet for twenty minutes in the specific way that meant he was thinking about something unrelated to the page in front of him.

"Have you ever been to Mirage Inc?" he said.

Jake looked up. "A couple of times. Why?"

Mike turned his laptop around. On the screen: Mirage Inc's public website, the version designed for people who could afford to want it. A full immersion simulation platform, the copy said. Not a game. An experience. The most sophisticated virtual reality environment ever built, accessible via authorized facilities across the country, delivering complete sensory immersion indistinguishable from physical reality.

The product was called The Mirage. Not a single environment but an entire simulated planet — built from scratch, running continuously, populated by AI-generated characters living AI-generated lives across dozens of distinct regions. Users entered through stasis pods, rented a body belonging to a local resident who had agreed to the arrangement, and woke up somewhere inside it as a real physical presence. A cyberpunk megacity. A medieval countryside. A coastal town that existed nowhere on real Earth but felt like somewhere you had always known. To be inside The Mirage was, by every account anyone had ever given, to simply be there.

The price for a weekend access package was listed on request only, which was the website's way of saying that if you had to ask you already knew the answer.

"I've been reading about it for two years," Mike said. "There's a forum community — people who've been inside as users, sharing what they saw. Not just one place. An entire world. Countryside, cities, coastlines, historical environments — all of it running simultaneously, all of it populated." He paused. "One person wrote that they spent a week in a village in Provence that doesn't exist anywhere on real Earth. Every person in it had a life that made complete sense from the inside. They said coming back was the hardest thing they'd ever done."

Jake looked at the screen. He had been twice — once when he was sixteen, once last year — both times briefly, both times with his father, both times with the vague sense that he was seeing only a small corner of something whose full scale he couldn't grasp. His father had seemed proud to show him and then slightly relieved when they left, as if the visit had satisfied an obligation he hadn't fully understood.

"It's impressive," Jake said.

"Impressive." Mike said it the same way he said things when he thought a word was significantly insufficient. "They built an entire planet. And the people who can really afford it — they don't just visit." He scrolled down the page. "They move in. Permanently. Full stasis, medical supervision, life support. Their bodies are maintained in pods while their minds are inside. Mirage Inc says the average client lives ten times longer than they would have outside." He looked up. "People are spending the last years of their lives in there. Except from the inside it doesn't feel like the last years of anything." He turned the laptop back. "I don't think impressive is the right word. I think it's the most interesting thing anyone has ever built."

"You want to go."

"I've wanted to go since I first read about it." He said it simply, the way he said things that were just true. "I know what it costs. I know I won't go. But yes."

Jake looked at his coffee cup. Then at his phone, face-down on the table, his father's last text still unanswered. He thought about the company name that had been in the background of his entire life, the way his father came home at six and never talked about it.

"Maybe there's a way," Jake said.

Mike looked at him. "What do you mean?"

"My dad works there. He has access slots sometimes — employee benefit things. I've never asked." He picked up his phone. "Let me see."

Mike was quiet for a moment. "You don't have to do that."

"I know." But Jake was already looking at his father's contact, thinking about it. He put the phone back down. "I'll think about it."

Arthur appeared in the doorway, having resolved whatever needed resolving. He looked at both of them, read the room in approximately one second, and went to the vending machine without asking what had happened. He bought three things — one for each of them, apparently by instinct — and put Jake's coffee and Mike's green tea on the table without comment and sat down with his own chips and opened his laptop.

The three of them sat in the common room and the evening went on around them.

---

The Cicada appeared in late October.

Mike found it, which made more sense than Jake finding it would have. Mike was the one who lived on the internet's stranger edges — the cryptography forums, the CTF communities, the archived threads where people documented puzzles that had no explanation and no stated reward. He had been part of those communities since he was fifteen and had taught himself to pick locks with forum guides and broken encryption schemes for fun during Kansas City summers with nothing else to do.

He showed it to Jake one evening without preamble, turning his ThinkPad around across the common room table.

A black image. In the center, rendered in fine white lines, a cicada — the insect, wings folded, perfectly detailed, the kind of illustration that belonged in a nineteenth century naturalist's notebook. Below it, a single line of text in a clean monospace font:

*The next emergence begins November 3. Those who can see what others cannot are invited to begin.*

Below that, a string of numbers.

"What is it?" Jake asked.

"I don't know yet." Mike turned the laptop back. "The number string isn't random. It has structure — encoded, not generated. And the image itself is too carefully made to be casual." He pulled up an archive thread alongside it. "There's a history of things like this. Anonymous puzzle sequences. No explanation, no reward stated. They require cryptography, steganography, lateral thinking — sometimes all at once."

"Who makes them?"

"Someone testing for specific skills. Or recruiting." Mike scrolled through the archive. "No one who has finished one has ever publicly said what they found at the end."

Jake looked at the illustration — the precise lines, the clean font, the number string with its almost-visible structure. "When does it start?"

"November third."

Arthur appeared from the hallway, caught the tail end of this, and leaned over to look at the screen. He studied it for a moment. "This is not a game, bro," he said.

"No," Mike agreed.

"What do you think is at the end of it?"

Mike was quiet for a moment. "Something real. You don't build this carefully for nothing."

Arthur looked at it a moment longer, nodded once as if filing it appropriately, and went to make something in the kitchenette. Jake looked at the image a while longer. Then he went back to what he had been doing.

They left it there. November third was two weeks away and they had midterms and problem sets and the ordinary weight of a first semester that wasn't slowing down for anything. The Cicada sat in a pinned tab on Mike's browser, patient, waiting for the third.

---

The invitation arrived on a Thursday in early November.

Jake got the notification at eleven in the morning, between lectures — a text from Berkeley Housing: *Package available for pickup at Unit 2 front desk. Bring your Cal ID.*

He picked it up that evening on the way back from the rec center, still in his workout clothes. The envelope was heavier than he expected, cream-colored, understated in the way that expensive things often were — no logo visible from the outside, just his name and the dorm address in clean print. The return address was a San Francisco office building. He recognized the street.

He carried it back to the room without opening it.

All three of them were there when he came in — Mike at his desk in his usual configuration, Arthur on his bed with his laptop, a video call running quietly in Armenian that he ended with a few words and a wave when Jake came through the door.

Jake set the envelope on his desk and looked at it for a moment.

"What is that?" Arthur asked.

"I think it's from my dad." Jake picked it up and opened it.

Inside: three smaller envelopes, each sealed, each with a name on the front. His own. Mike Bondarenko. Arthur Mkhitaryan.

He looked up. Mike had turned around from his desk. Arthur had sat up on his bed.

"He sent three," Jake said.

He passed them out. His own first, then Mike's across the room, then Arthur's. They opened them more or less simultaneously — Jake first, Arthur half a second later, Mike with the deliberate care of someone who opened things carefully as a rule.

Inside each: a single card, heavy stock, the Mirage Inc name embossed at the top in a way that was visible only when the light caught it at an angle. An invitation to the company's annual Open Doors event — one day in which a small number of guests were invited to experience The Mirage as users, hosted by Mirage Inc staff, with full access to the standard user environments for a six-hour session. The date was three weeks away. A Saturday.

At the bottom of Jake's card, in his father's handwriting: *Thought you'd want to bring your roommates. Looking forward to showing you around properly. — Dad.*

The room was quiet for a moment.

"Vay qu ara," Arthur said quietly, still looking at his card. Then, louder: "This is Mirage Inc. The real one."

"He knew our names," Mike said. He was looking at his card with the focused attention he gave to things that had surprised him, which wasn't many things.

"I must have mentioned you both." Jake hadn't, consciously. But his father had a way of knowing things without being told.

Arthur was reading every line of his invitation with the thoroughness of someone reviewing a legal document. "The full experience this is? The stasis pods, the simulation — everything?"

"I think so. The real thing."

Arthur looked up. Something had shifted in his expression — not the open enthusiasm he brought to most things, something more careful. "My parents will want to know about this," he said, mostly to himself. Then he looked at Jake. "Your father — he knows my name. How does he know my name, ape?" He caught himself. "Bro. Sorry."

Mike set his card down on his desk beside his keyboard. He looked at it for a moment, then looked at Jake. The expression was the one Jake had learned to read over two months of close quarters — the one that meant something had landed somewhere significant and Mike was deciding how much of that to show.

"Thank you," he said. "Tell your dad thank you."

"Tell him yourself," Jake said. "He'll be there."

Mike nodded and turned back to his desk. Arthur looked at his invitation one more time. "I will need to buy better shoes," he said, to no one in particular. Jake looked at his own invitation — the embossed name catching the light, the date three weeks away, his father's handwriting at the bottom. He set it against the wall where he could see it from his bed.

Across the room Mike's laptop screen was still open to the pinned tab. The Cicada image sat there, patient and precise, waiting for November third.

Two weeks from now they would stand in a facility in San Francisco and lie down in pods and wake up somewhere that shouldn't exist.

Jake looked at the invitation. Then at the Cicada. Then out the window at the Berkeley night, the city spread below the hills, ordinary and familiar and exactly what it appeared to be.

For now.
